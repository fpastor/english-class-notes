## Describing lives in the past

When we describe people's lives in the past we can use the following options:  
  
**Simple Past** or **Present Perfect Tense.**
  
But **when** do we use each of them?

## When did it happen?

Use the **Simple Past Tense** when we want to describe something that **started** in the past and **ended** in the past:  
  
I **got** my bachelor's degree **in 1998.**  
I **got** my first job **two years later.**  
I **quit** my previous job **last year.**

## Specific reference in the past

**Read the following examples. All these sentences make reference to a one-point action in the past.**

When you want to express when something happened (in the past), you can refer to:

- a specific century / decade / year / month / day  

I went to school **in the nineties.**
I had my first son **in 2001.**
I finished my last project **in May.**
I had a very important board meeting **on Monday.**
  
*in the nineties*, *in 2001*, *in May*, and *on Monday* are time expressions commonly used in the Simple Past Tense.

## General time in the past

You can also use a general pastime expression as in the following examples:  
  
- I got my MBA **two years ago.**
- I started to go to the gym **last month.**
- I'm very tired today. **Yesterday** was a very busy day.

## Do you know when it happened?

We can use **Present Perfect Tense:**
When the date it happened is **not** important or known.

**I've left** my previous job. = Time is not important, only the event.
My roommate **has moved** to a new apartment. = Time is not important, only the event.

> Remember! There is **no** definite time reference!

## Affirmative statements in the Present Perfect Tense

**Notice that we use have / has + past participle.**

> For affirmative statements we can use:

For subjects I, You, We, or They:
I **have worked** here for three years. = **have + past participle**
  
For subjects She, He or It:
He **has lived** in this city since last month. = **has + past participle**

## Negative Statements in the Present Perfect Tense

> For negative statements we can use:  
  
For subjects I, You, We, or They:
They **haven't worked** here for three years. = **have + not + past** participle    
For subjects He, She or It:
She **hasn't lived** in this city since last month. = **has + not + past participle**

## Questions in the Present Perfect Tense

For questions we can use:  
  
For subjects I, You, We or They:
**Have** you **worked** here for three years? = **Have + subject + past participle**
*Yes,* **I have.**
*No,* **I haven't.**
  
For subjects She, He or It:
**Has** he **lived** in this city since last month? = **Has + subject + past participle**
*Yes, he* **has.**
*No, he* **hasn't.**

## Time reference: for a period of time

We use **for** + a period of time.  
  
I've been married **for twenty years.**
We've worked on our project **for three months.**
My brother has studied medicine **for four years.**

## Time reference: since a specific time in the past

We use **since** + a specific time in the past.  
  
I've worked in this company **since 2010.**
My wife has lived in this country **since last year.**
We've worked on this project **since May.**

## For and since: examples

**These are some of the possible expressions with for an since**

If somebody says: I've worked here for ten years, it means that the person started working there ten years ago, but is still working there.

|for|since|
|---|---|
|I haven't heard from you for ages.|I haven't heard from you since you left the office.|
|I've worked on the project for 2 years.|I've worked on the project since 2017.|
|She'd been my assistant for a month.|She's been my assistant since June.|
Use: for + period of time Use: since + specific time in the past
